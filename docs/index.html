Methodology

What is AVO?

AVO (Amplitude Variation with Offset) is the industry-standard technique used by oil companies worldwide. It analyzes how seismic reflections change when viewed from different angles.

Real-world analogy: Like looking at a lake from different angles - the reflection changes based on your viewing position. These changes tell us about what's beneath the surface.

Why it works: Gas-filled rocks and water-filled rocks reflect seismic waves differently at different angles, allowing us to detect hydrocarbons.

Amplitude Variation with Offset (AVO) modeling computes angle-dependent reflectivity at multiple incident angles (0°, 5°, 10°, 15°) using the Zoeppritz equations. The approach exploits amplitude variations to infer lithology and fluid properties, with the full-stack seismogram generated by combining all angle gathers into a composite trace.

Mathematical Framework

Based on the Aki-Richards linearization of Zoeppritz equations for P-wave reflection coefficients:

Understanding the Formula

Aki-Richards Approximation simplifies the complex Zoeppritz equations into a linear relationship between reflection amplitude and angle. Think of it as breaking down seismic reflections into two components:

  • R₀ (Intercept): The reflection strength when looking straight down (0° angle) - tells us about basic rock contrasts
  • G (Gradient): How much the reflection changes as the angle increases - the key to detecting fluids like gas or oil

The sin 2 ( θ ) term means changes are more dramatic at larger angles, which is why far-offset data is valuable for fluid detection.

Aki-Richards Approximation
R ( θ ) ≈ R 0 + G · sin 2 ( θ )
R 0 = intercept (normal incidence reflectivity)
G = gradient (AVO gradient)
θ = incident angle

Strengths

  • Industry-standard technique
  • Physically rigorous (Zoeppritz equations)
  • Multiple angles provide fluid sensitivity
  • Well-understood interpretation workflow
  • Quality weighting optimization implemented

Limitations

  • Moderate facies discrimination (Cohen's d = 0.474 )
  • Poor gradient correlation (Pearson r = 0.003 )
  • Requires accurate velocity model
  • Computational complexity (4 angle volumes)
  • Sensitive to linearization assumptions

The fundamental discrimination limitation persists across domains: gradient correlation r ≈ 0.003 remains negligible in time domain, and Cohen's d = 0.474 proves inadequate for quantitative reservoir characterization. This reflects the inherent two-parameter constraint of conventional P-wave AVO analysis—the methodology cannot access the three-dimensional elastic parameter space ( V p , V s , ρ ) required for robust facies separation, regardless of depth-time coordinate transformation.

Description

What is Acoustic Impedance?

Acoustic Impedance (AI) is the simplest seismic property - it only uses two measurements: how fast sound travels ( V p ) and how dense the rock is ( ρ ).

Advantages: Fast to compute, well understood, requires only basic measurements

Limitation: Only looks at the rock from straight down (0° angle), missing the angle-dependent information that reveals fluids. It also ignores shear waves ( V s ) which are crucial for distinguishing rock types.

AI Synthetic Seismogram modeling uses normal-incidence reflectivity derived from acoustic impedance to generate synthetic seismic traces. AI (Acoustic Impedance) is the product of P-wave velocity ( V p ) and density ( ρ ), providing a fundamental measure of rock mechanical properties. This is the simplest and most widely used seismic modeling approach.

Physics & Mathematics

Understanding the Formula

Acoustic Impedance (AI) is the simplest seismic property - just multiply two rock measurements together:

  • V p (P-wave velocity): How fast compressional sound waves travel through the rock (meters/second)
  • ρ (Density): How heavy the rock is (grams/cubic centimeter)

Reflection Coefficient (RC): When a seismic wave hits a boundary between two rock layers, the RC tells us how much energy bounces back. It's calculated from the impedance contrast - bigger contrasts create stronger reflections.

AI = V p × ρ
RC = AI ₂ - AI ₁ AI ₂ + AI ₁

Why it's limited: Using only two parameters means you can't tell the difference between a dense slow rock and a light fast rock if they have the same AI!

Acoustic Impedance is defined as:

Acoustic Impedance
AI = V p × ρ
RC = (AI₂ - AI₁) / (AI₂ + AI₁)
V p = P-wave velocity
ρ = density
RC = reflection coefficient

Reflectivity is computed from impedance contrasts and convolved with a Ricker wavelet to generate synthetic seismograms.

Strengths

  • Simplest and fastest technique
  • Direct physical interpretation
  • Well-log calibration straightforward
  • Minimal computational requirements
  • Stable and robust results

Limitations

  • Similar performance to Full-Stack (Cohen's d = 0.470 )
  • No angle dependence (misses fluid effects)
  • Limited lithology sensitivity
  • Only uses V p and ρ (ignores V s )
  • Poor facies discrimination

This parsimony, while advantageous for rapid seismic-to-well correlation and preliminary stratigraphic interpretation, fundamentally constrains discriminatory capacity. Lithofacies exhibiting disparate elastic moduli but fortuitously similar P-impedances become seismically indistinguishable under this formulation.

The modest effect size underscores AI's fundamental constraint: as a two-parameter composite, it cannot uniquely resolve the three-parameter elastic space required for reliable lithological classification. While time-domain AI synthetics facilitate seismic-to-well ties and horizon interpretation, their utility for quantitative facies prediction remains severely limited by absent shear wave sensitivity and velocity model uncertainties.

Physics & Mathematics

Understanding the Formula

Elastic Impedance (EI) combines three rock properties:

  • V p : P-wave velocity (how fast compressional waves travel)
  • V s : S-wave velocity (how fast shear waves travel)
  • ρ : Density (how heavy the rock is)

The angle ( θ ) determines how much each property influences the result. Different angles are sensitive to different aspects of the rock - some angles see fluids better, others see lithology changes better.

Angle-dependent Elastic Impedance (Connolly, 1999):

Elastic Impedance (Connolly, 1999)
EI ( θ ) = V p 1 + tan 2 θ · V s - 8 K · sin 2 θ · ρ 1 - 4 K · sin 2 θ
K = ( V s / V p )² = velocity ratio squared
θ = incidence angle
Optimal Stack = Σ w i · EI ( θ i )
w i = var ( EI i ) Σ j var ( EI j ) = variance-based weight

The depth-domain impedance analysis works directly with rock properties, achieving 157.9 × better gradient correlation than AVO and 26.8 × improvement over time-domain seismograms (Cohen's d = 14.046 vs 0.524 ).

Why Multiple Angles? Each angle provides complementary information. By combining 6 angles (0° through 25°) with smart weighting, we get more robust and accurate results than any single angle could provide.

Facies Discrimination Analysis

What is Cohen's d?

Cohen's d is a standardized measure of effect size that quantifies the separation between two distributions. In our study, it measures how well we can distinguish between different rock types (facies).

Simple Explanation: Imagine two groups of rocks. Cohen's d tells us how far apart their seismic responses are. A larger value means it's easier to tell them apart - which is exactly what geologists need for reservoir characterization.

Understanding the Formula

Cohen's d measures how separated two groups are in units of standard deviation. It's like asking: "How many standard deviations apart are these two rock types?"

  • μ 1 - μ 2 (Numerator): The difference between average seismic responses of two facies - bigger difference means easier to distinguish
  • σ pooled (Denominator): Combined variability within each group - less variability means clearer separation

Interpretation: d = 0.5 means groups overlap significantly. d = 3.0 means almost no overlap. d = 14.0 (our EI result) means virtually perfect separation!

Cohen's d Effect Size
d = μ 1 - μ 2 σ pooled
where:
μ₁, μ₂ = mean values for two facies
σpooled = combined standard deviation

Interpretation Guidelines

Cohen's d Effect Size Interpretation Technique
0.2 - 0.5 Small Minimal discrimination -
0.5 - 0.8 Medium Moderate discrimination Full-Stack, AI Synthetic
0.8 - 1.2 Large Good discrimination -
> 3.0 HUGE Excellent discrimination Multi-Angle EI (14.046)

While this interactive time-domain visualization serves essential functions in standard seismic workflows, its utility for high-confidence quantitative reservoir characterization remains fundamentally limited—a constraint visualized through direct 3D comparison with the superior depth-domain impedance platform.

Lambda-Rho (λρ) - Fluid Sensitive

Incompressibility × Density - Best pure rock physics attribute for fluid detection with Cohen's d: 14.89 (HUGE effect).

Figure 8. Distribution of Lambda-Rho values for each facies type.

Technical Analysis

Lambda-Rho (λρ) is a cornerstone of modern rock physics, originating from the Lambda-Mu-Rho (LMR) decomposition proposed by Goodway et al. (1997). This technique transforms elastic parameters ( V p , V s , ρ ) into a domain where fluid and lithology effects are more clearly separated. Lambda (λ), Lamé's first parameter, represents the rock's incompressibility—its resistance to a change in volume. Since fluids have no shear strength ( μ = 0 ) , their entire elastic response is captured by their incompressibility, making any attribute based on Lambda (λ) highly sensitive to pore fluid changes. The Stanford VI-E paper explicitly mentions the use of Lame's parameters as seismic attributes to discriminate lithology and detect hydrocarbons, following the work of Goodway et al. (1997).

The attribute is calculated as λρ = ( ρ V p 2 - 2 ρ V s 2 ) × ρ Here, ρ V p 2 is the P-wave modulus (M), and ρVs2 is the shear modulus (μ). The formula essentially isolates the non-shear component of the rock's stiffness and scales it by density to have units of impedance. This makes it directly comparable to other seismic attributes. The calculation is performed in src/rock_physics_attributes.py using the `compute_lambda_mu_rho` function.

In the context of the Stanford VI-E dataset, this plot demonstrates the exceptional power of λρ as a Direct Hydrocarbon Indicator (DHI). The dataset includes brine-saturated sands (Facies 2) and oil-saturated sands (Facies 3). As stated in the paper, the oil has a bulk modulus of 0.5 GPa, while the brine has a bulk modulus of 2.57 GPa. Because oil is far more compressible than brine, the oil-bearing sands exhibit significantly lower λρ values. This results in a clear vertical separation on the crossplot and a Cohen's d of 14.89, which indicates a "HUGE" effect size with virtually no overlap between the fluid-filled facies distributions. While powerful, it's important to note that the accuracy of λρ is highly dependent on the quality of the input Vp, Vs, and density data, with errors in Vs estimation being a common source of uncertainty in real-world applications. The paper also highlights that random variability was added to the elastic moduli to make the synthetic data more realistic, which can affect the separation of the clusters.

Mu-Rho (μρ) - Lithology Sensitive

Shear modulus × Density - Separates rock matrix (shale vs sand) with Cohen's d: 2.58 (VERY LARGE effect).

Figure 9. Distribution of Mu-Rho values for each facies type.

Technical Analysis

Mu-Rho (μρ) is the second key component of the LMR decomposition, representing the rock's rigidity. It is the product of the shear modulus (μ) and density (ρ). The shear modulus, or rigidity, measures a rock's resistance to shear stress (a change in shape). A critical principle of rock physics is that fluids cannot be sheared and have a shear modulus of zero. Therefore, the μρ attribute is almost entirely insensitive to the pore fluid and is instead dominated by the properties of the solid rock matrix. The Stanford VI-E paper confirms that the Lame parameter μ is not much sensitive to the change in saturation.

This makes μρ an excellent lithology indicator. The calculation, μρ = ( ρ V s 2 ) × ρ , is driven by the S-wave velocity (Vs), which travels through the rock frame. As shown in the plot, Mu-Rho effectively separates the sandstones (Facies 2 and 3) from the shales (Facies 0 and 1). The sandstones, with their more rigid quartz framework, have higher μρ values, while the less rigid, clay-rich shales have lower values. This horizontal separation on a λρ-μρ crossplot is the classic signature of a lithology change. The `compute_lambda_mu_rho` function in `src/rock_physics_attributes.py` performs this calculation.

The reported Cohen's d of 2.58 for this attribute signifies a "VERY LARGE" effect size, confirming its strong capability for lithology discrimination. However, the accuracy of μρ is critically dependent on the S-wave velocity (Vs) measurement, which is often the most challenging elastic parameter to acquire and process, making it a potential source of uncertainty. The paper mentions that S-wave velocities were derived from P-wave velocities using empirical relations from Castagna et al. (1985, 1993), which is a common practice but can introduce errors if the rock properties deviate from the empirical trend.

Fluid Factor

λρ-μρ separation - Direct hydrocarbon indicator that removes lithology effects with Cohen's d: 9.51 (HUGE effect).

Figure 10. The Fluid Factor isolates fluid effects by subtracting lithology-sensitive Mu-Rho (μρ) from fluid-sensitive Lambda-Rho (λρ), creating a direct hydrocarbon indicator.

Technical Analysis

The Fluid Factor is a hybrid rock physics attribute designed to isolate the pore fluid response by explicitly removing the influence of the rock matrix. It is derived from the LMR parameters by subtracting a scaled portion of the lithology-sensitive Mu-Rho (μρ) from the fluid-sensitive Lambda-Rho (λρ). The goal is to find a projection in the λρ-μρ space that is orthogonal to the primary lithology trend, thus making the resulting attribute sensitive only to fluid changes.

The formula used is Fluid   Factor = λρ - c × μρ , where the constant 'c' represents the slope of the background lithology trend (e.g., the brine-saturated sandstone trend) in the λρ-μρ crossplot. In src/rock_physics_attributes.py, a constant c=1.0 is used as a first-order approximation. By removing the lithology component, the Fluid Factor effectively becomes a Direct Hydrocarbon Indicator (DHI).

As shown in Figure 10, the Fluid Factor shows a very strong separation between the oil-saturated sand (F3) and the brine-saturated sand (F2), making it an excellent DHI. The negative values for the oil sand are a direct consequence of the lower incompressibility of oil compared to brine. The attribute achieves a Cohen's d of 9.51, a "HUGE" effect size, confirming its effectiveness.

Poisson Impedance

Fluid-sensitive impedance using Poisson's ratio with Cohen's d: 0.91 (LARGE effect).

Figure 11. Distribution of Poisson Impedance values for each facies type.

Technical Analysis

Poisson Impedance (PI), introduced by Quakenbush et al. (2006), is an elastic attribute designed to be sensitive to variations in Poisson's ratio (σ). Poisson's ratio itself is a fundamental elastic property that describes the transverse strain of a material relative to its axial strain and is closely related to the V p V s . Because the V p V s ratio is highly sensitive to pore fluids (e.g., gas-saturated rocks exhibit low V p V s and low Poisson's ratio), PI can be an effective fluid indicator.

The formula for Poisson Impedance is PI = AI × ( 1 - σ ) . As seen in src/rock_physics_attributes.py, it is calculated from Vp, Vs, and density. The plot in Figure 11 demonstrates that Poisson Impedance provides a good separation between the different facies, particularly between the sand and shale units. While not as powerful as Lambda-Rho or the Fluid Factor in this specific dataset, it serves as another valuable tool for fluid and lithology discrimination, achieving a Cohen's d of 0.91 (a "LARGE" effect size).

Classic Rock Physics - λ-μ Crossplot

The Goodway (1997) decomposition separates fluid effects (λρ - vertical) from lithology effects (μρ - horizontal).

Figure 12. Rock physics crossplot for facies separation. The vertical axis, Lambda-Rho (λρ), is sensitive to fluid content and distinguishes brine from oil. The horizontal axis, Mu-Rho (μρ), is sensitive to lithology and separates shale from sand.

Technical Analysis

This crossplot (Figure 12) is a cornerstone of modern rock physics analysis,based on the Goodway et al. (1997) Lambda-Mu-Rho (LMR) decomposition . The technique transforms seismic data from P-wave and S-wave impedances into Lamé parameters, which have a more direct physical meaning. The crossplot of Lambda-Rho (λρ) versus Mu-Rho (μρ) is particularly powerful because it is designed to orthogonally separate lithology effects from pore fluid effects.

Mu-Rho (μρ), plotted on the x-axis, represents the rock's rigidity. Since fluids do not support shear stress, this attribute is primarily sensitive to the solid rock matrix. This is why it is an excellent lithology indicator. In the plot, we can see a clear horizontal separation between the less rigid shales (F0, F1) on the left and the more rigid sandstones (F2, F3) on the right.

Lambda-Rho (λρ), plotted on the y-axis, represents the rock's incompressibility. This parameter is sensitive to both the rock matrix and the pore fluid. However, when comparing rocks of the same lithology (i.e., at a constant μρ), the primary driver of λρ variations is the fluid content. This is evident in the vertical separation between the oil-saturated sand (F3), which has lower incompressibility and thus lower λρ, and the brine-saturated sand (F2). This orthogonal relationship is the key to the LMR technique's success in discriminating lithology and fluids simultaneously.

This plot can be used for quantitative interpretation by defining polygons or probability density functions for each facies cluster. These can then be used to classify unknown data points, providing a powerful tool for creating detailed lithology and fluid maps of a reservoir.
